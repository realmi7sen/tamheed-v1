## Problem

Evaluate the integral:
(a) $\int x^2 e^{3x^3} \, dx$
(b) $\int_1^2 x^2 e^{3x^3} \, dx$

---

## Rule Used

**Exponential Integration Rule**
$$\int e^u \, du = e^u + C$$

**The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part II)**
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

---

## Solution

### Part (a)

**Step 1 — Perform substitution**
Let $u = 3x^3$. Then $du = 9x^2 \, dx \implies x^2 \, dx = \frac{1}{9} du$.
Substitute into the integral:
$$\int x^2 e^{3x^3} \, dx = \int e^u \cdot \frac{1}{9} du = \frac{1}{9} \int e^u \, du$$

**Step 2 — Integrate and substitute back**
$$\frac{1}{9} e^u + C = \frac{1}{9} e^{3x^3} + C$$

---

### Part (b)

**Step 1 — Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus**
$$\int_1^2 x^2 e^{3x^3} \, dx = \left[ \frac{1}{9} e^{3x^3} \right]_1^2$$

**Step 2 — Evaluate at the limits**
Upper limit ($x = 2$):
$$F(2) = \frac{1}{9} e^{3(2)^3} = \frac{1}{9} e^{24}$$

Lower limit ($x = 1$):
$$F(1) = \frac{1}{9} e^{3(1)^3} = \frac{1}{9} e^3$$

**Step 3 — Subtract and factor**
$$F(2) - F(1) = \frac{1}{9} e^{24} - \frac{1}{9} e^3 = \frac{1}{9} (e^{24} - e^3)$$

---

## Answer

(a) $\frac{1}{9} e^{3x^3} + C$
(b) $\frac{1}{9} (e^{24} - e^3)$

---

## Check by Differentiation (Part a)

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{1}{9} e^{3x^3} \right] = \frac{1}{9} e^{3x^3} \cdot (9x^2) = x^2 e^{3x^3} \quad \checkmark$$

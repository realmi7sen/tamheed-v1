## Problem

Evaluate the integral:
(a) $\int \frac{e^x}{1 + e^{2x}} \, dx$
(b) $\int_0^1 \frac{e^x}{1 + e^{2x}} \, dx$

---

## Rule Used

**Inverse Tangent Integration Formula**
$$\int \frac{1}{1 + u^2} \, du = \tan^{-1}(u) + C$$

**The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part II)**
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

---

## Solution

### Part (a)

**Step 1 — Perform substitution**
Rewrite $e^{2x}$ as $(e^x)^2$.
Let $u = e^x$. Then $du = e^x \, dx$.

**Step 2 — Substitute and integrate**
$$\int \frac{e^x}{1 + (e^x)^2} \, dx = \int \frac{1}{1 + u^2} \, du = \tan^{-1}(u) + C$$

**Step 3 — Substitute back**
$$\tan^{-1}(e^x) + C$$

---

### Part (b)

**Step 1 — Apply FTC Part II**
$$\int_0^1 \frac{e^x}{1 + e^{2x}} \, dx = \left[ \tan^{-1}(e^x) \right]_0^1$$

**Step 2 — Evaluate at limits**
Upper limit ($x = 1$):
$$F(1) = \tan^{-1}(e^1) = \tan^{-1}(e)$$

Lower limit ($x = 0$):
$$F(0) = \tan^{-1}(e^0) = \tan^{-1}(1) = \frac{\pi}{4}$$

**Step 3 — Subtract**
$$F(1) - F(0) = \tan^{-1}(e) - \frac{\pi}{4}$$

---

## Answer

(a) $\tan^{-1}(e^x) + C$
(b) $\tan^{-1}(e) - \frac{\pi}{4}$

---

## Check by Differentiation (Part a)

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \tan^{-1}(e^x) \right] = \frac{1}{1 + (e^x)^2} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = \frac{e^x}{1 + e^{2x}} \quad \checkmark$$

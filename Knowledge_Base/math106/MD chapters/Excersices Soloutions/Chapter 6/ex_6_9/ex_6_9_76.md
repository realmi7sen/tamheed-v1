## Problem

Find the limit, if it exists.
$$\lim_{x \to -3^-} \left(\frac{x}{x^2 + 2x - 3} - \frac{4}{x + 3}\right)$$

---

## Rule Used

**L'Hôpital's Rule / Algebraic Simplification**
If a limit presents an indeterminate form of type $[\infty - \infty]$, combine the fractions over a common denominator first.

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Factor the denominators**
Notice that $x^2 + 2x - 3 = (x + 3)(x - 1)$.

**Step 2 — Combine the fractions**
$$\frac{x}{(x + 3)(x - 1)} - \frac{4}{x + 3} = \frac{x - 4(x - 1)}{(x + 3)(x - 1)} = \frac{x - 4x + 4}{(x + 3)(x - 1)} = \frac{-3x + 4}{(x + 3)(x - 1)}$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate limit by direct substitution**
Analyze the limit as $x \to -3^-$:
- Numerator: $-3(-3) + 4 = 9 + 4 = 13$
- Denominator factor $(x - 1)$: $-3 - 1 = -4$
- Denominator factor $(x + 3)$: As $x \to -3^-$, $(x + 3) \to 0^-$ (negative values)

So the denominator approach:
$$(x + 3)(x - 1) \to (0^-)(-4) = 0^+$$

Therefore:
$$\lim_{x \to -3^-} \frac{-3x + 4}{(x + 3)(x - 1)} = \frac{13}{0^+} = +\infty$$

---

## Answer

$$\infty$$

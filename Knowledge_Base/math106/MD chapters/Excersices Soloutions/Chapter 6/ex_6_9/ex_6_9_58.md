## Problem

Find the limit, if it exists.
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} (e^x + 3x)^{1/x}$$

---

## Rule Used

**L'Hôpital's Rule**
If $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ yields the indeterminate form $\left[\frac{0}{0}\right]$, then:
$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Identify the indeterminate form**
As $x \to 0^+$, $e^x + 3x \to 1 + 0 = 1$ and $\frac{1}{x} \to \infty$.
This gives the indeterminate form $1^\infty$.

**Step 2 — Take the natural logarithm**
Let $y = (e^x + 3x)^{1/x}$.
$$\ln y = \frac{\ln(e^x + 3x)}{x}$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln y$**
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln(e^x + 3x)}{x} \quad \left[\frac{0}{0}\right]$$

Apply L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{d}{dx}[\ln(e^x + 3x)]}{\frac{d}{dx}[x]} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{e^x + 3}{e^x + 3x}}{1} = \frac{e^0 + 3}{e^0 + 0} = \frac{1 + 3}{1} = 4$$

**Step 4 — Exponentiate to find the original limit**
Since $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln y = 4$:
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} y = e^4$$

---

## Answer

$$e^4$$

## Problem

Find the limit, if it exists.
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x$$

---

## Rule Used

**L'Hôpital's Rule**
If $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ yields the indeterminate form $\left[\frac{0}{0}\right]$ or $\left[\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right]$, then:
$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Identify the indeterminate form**
Direct substitution gives:
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x = 0 \cdot (-\infty) \quad [0 \cdot \infty \text{ form}]$$

**Step 2 — Transform to $\left[\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right]$ form**
Rewrite the product as a quotient by sending $x$ to the denominator as $\frac{1}{x}$:
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln x}{\frac{1}{x}} \quad \left[\frac{-\infty}{\infty}\right]$$

**Step 3 — Apply L'Hôpital's Rule**
Differentiate the numerator and denominator with respect to $x$:
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x]}{\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{1}{x}\right]} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\frac{1}{x}}{-\frac{1}{x^2}}$$

**Step 4 — Simplify and evaluate**
$$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \left( \frac{1}{x} \cdot (-x^2) \right) = \lim_{x \to 0^+} (-x) = 0$$

---

## Answer

$$0$$

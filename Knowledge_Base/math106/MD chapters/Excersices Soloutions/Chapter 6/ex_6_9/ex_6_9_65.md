## Problem

Find the limit, if it exists.
$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} \left(\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{\sin x}\right)$$

---

## Rule Used

**L'Hôpital's Rule**
If $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ yields the indeterminate form $\left[\frac{0}{0}\right]$, then:
$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Identify the indeterminate form**
As $x \to 0^-$, $\frac{1}{x} \to -\infty$ and $\frac{1}{\sin x} \to -\infty$.
This yields the indeterminate form $[-\infty - (-\infty)] = [\infty - \infty]$.

**Step 2 — Combine over a common denominator**
$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{\sin x - x}{x \sin x} \quad \left[\frac{0}{0}\right]$$

**Step 3 — Apply L'Hôpital's Rule (1st time)**
Differentiate top and bottom with respect to $x$:
$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{\cos x - 1}{\sin x + x \cos x} \quad \left[\frac{0}{0}\right]$$

**Step 4 — Apply L'Hôpital's Rule (2nd time)**
$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{-\sin x}{\cos x + (\cos x - x \sin x)} = \lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{-\sin x}{2\cos x - x \sin x}$$

**Step 5 — Evaluate by direct substitution**
$$\frac{-\sin(0)}{2\cos(0) - 0 \cdot \sin(0)} = \frac{0}{2(1) - 0} = 0$$

---

## Answer

$$0$$

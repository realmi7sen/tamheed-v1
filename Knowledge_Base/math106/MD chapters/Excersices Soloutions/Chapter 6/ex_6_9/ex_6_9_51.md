## Problem

Find the limit, if it exists.
$$\lim_{x \to \infty} (x^2 - 1)e^{-x^2}$$

---

## Rule Used

**L'Hôpital's Rule**
If $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ yields the indeterminate form $\left[\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right]$, then:
$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Identify the indeterminate form**
As $x \to \infty$, $x^2 - 1 \to \infty$ and $e^{-x^2} \to 0$.
$$\lim_{x \to \infty} (x^2 - 1)e^{-x^2} \quad [\infty \cdot 0 \text{ form}]$$

**Step 2 — Rewrite as a fraction**
$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2 - 1}{e^{x^2}} \quad \left[\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right]$$

**Step 3 — Apply L'Hôpital's Rule**
Differentiate numerator and denominator:
$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x}{2x e^{x^2}}$$

**Step 4 — Simplify and evaluate**
Cancel $2x$ in the numerator and denominator:
$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{e^{x^2}} = \frac{1}{\infty} = 0$$

---

## Answer

$$0$$

## Problem

Use implicit differentiation to find $y'$.
$$e^{xy} - x^3 + 3y^2 = 11$$

---

## Rule Used

**Derivative of the Exponential Function (Chain Rule)**
$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^u] = e^u \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

**Product Rule**
$$\frac{d}{dx}[xy] = 1 \cdot y + x \cdot y' = y + xy'$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$**
Apply the chain rule and product rule to the term $e^{xy}$:
$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^{xy}] - \frac{d}{dx}[x^3] + \frac{d}{dx}[3y^2] = \frac{d}{dx}[11]$$
$$e^{xy}(y + xy') - 3x^2 + 6yy' = 0$$

**Step 2 — Expand and distribute**
Distribute $e^{xy}$ across $(y + xy')$:
$$y e^{xy} + x e^{xy} y' - 3x^2 + 6yy' = 0$$

**Step 3 — Isolate terms with $y'$**
Keep terms containing $y'$ on the left side and move the other terms to the right side:
$$x e^{xy} y' + 6yy' = 3x^2 - y e^{xy}$$

**Step 4 — Factor out $y'$ and solve**
Factor $y'$ on the left side:
$$y'(x e^{xy} + 6y) = 3x^2 - y e^{xy}$$
Divide by $(x e^{xy} + 6y)$ to solve for $y'$:
$$y' = \frac{3x^2 - y e^{xy}}{x e^{xy} + 6y}$$

---

## Answer

$$y' = \frac{3x^2 - y e^{xy}}{x e^{xy} + 6y}$$

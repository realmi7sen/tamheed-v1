## Problem
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{x}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}} \, dx$.

## Rule Used
**Algebraic substitution (power rule)** — this is the section's own warning in action: a trigonometric substitution ($x = 2\sin\theta$) works, but the presence of the factor $x\,dx$ makes the simple substitution $u = 4 - x^2$ much faster. Always check for the simpler method first.

## Solution
**Step 1 — Substitute.** Let $u = 4 - x^2$, $du = -2x \, dx$, so $x \, dx = -\tfrac{1}{2}du$:
$$\int \frac{x}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}} \, dx = -\frac{1}{2}\int u^{-1/2} \, du$$

**Step 2 — Integrate by the power rule.**
$$= -\frac{1}{2}\cdot 2u^{1/2} + C = -\sqrt{u} + C$$

**Step 3 — Return to $x$.**

## Answer
$$\int \frac{x}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}} \, dx = -\sqrt{4 - x^2} + C$$

## Check by Differentiation
$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[-(4 - x^2)^{1/2}\right] = -\frac{-2x}{2\sqrt{4 - x^2}} = \frac{x}{\sqrt{4 - x^2}} \checkmark$$

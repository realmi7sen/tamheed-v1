## Problem
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_4^9 \frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + 4} \, dx$.

## Rule Used
**Miscellaneous substitution** $u = \sqrt{x}$ with **change of limits** for the definite integral, then long division of the resulting rational integrand.

## Solution
**Step 1 — Substitute and change limits.** Let $u = \sqrt{x}$, so $x = u^2$, $dx = 2u \, du$.
Limits: $x = 4 \Rightarrow u = 2$; $\;x = 9 \Rightarrow u = 3$.
$$\int_4^9 \frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + 4} \, dx = \int_2^3 \frac{2u}{u + 4} \, du$$

**Step 2 — Divide.**
$$\frac{2u}{u + 4} = 2 - \frac{8}{u + 4}$$

**Step 3 — Integrate and evaluate.**
$$\int_2^3 \left(2 - \frac{8}{u + 4}\right) du = \Big[2u - 8\ln(u + 4)\Big]_2^3 = (6 - 8\ln 7) - (4 - 8\ln 6)$$

## Answer
$$\int_4^9 \frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + 4} \, dx = 2 - 8\ln\frac{7}{6} \approx 0.767$$

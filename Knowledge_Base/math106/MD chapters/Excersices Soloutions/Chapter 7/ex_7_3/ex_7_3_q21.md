## Problem
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{(4 + x^2)^2}{x^3} \, dx$.

## Rule Used
**Algebraic expansion + power rule.** No radical appears, so no trigonometric substitution is needed — expand the numerator and divide term by term. (This exercise exists to test whether you reach for the simpler method.)

## Solution
**Step 1 — Expand and divide.**
$$\frac{(4 + x^2)^2}{x^3} = \frac{16 + 8x^2 + x^4}{x^3} = 16x^{-3} + \frac{8}{x} + x$$

**Step 2 — Integrate term by term.**
$$\int \left(16x^{-3} + \frac{8}{x} + x\right) dx = 16\cdot\frac{x^{-2}}{-2} + 8\ln|x| + \frac{x^2}{2} + C$$

## Answer
$$\int \frac{(4 + x^2)^2}{x^3} \, dx = -\frac{8}{x^2} + 8\ln|x| + \frac{x^2}{2} + C$$

## Check by Differentiation
$$\frac{d}{dx}[\,\cdot\,] = \frac{16}{x^3} + \frac{8}{x} + x = \frac{16 + 8x^2 + x^4}{x^3} = \frac{(4+x^2)^2}{x^3} \checkmark$$

## Problem
Determine whether $\displaystyle\int_0^{\infty} \frac{\cos x}{1 + \sin^2 x} \, dx$ converges or diverges; if it converges, find its value.

## Rule Used
**Definition of the improper integral** + the substitution $u = \sin x$ (Example 6 of Section 7.5 gives the antiderivative $\tan^{-1}(\sin x)$).

## Solution
**Step 1 — Find the antiderivative.** With $u = \sin x$, $du = \cos x\,dx$:
$$\int \frac{\cos x}{1 + \sin^2 x} \, dx = \int \frac{1}{1 + u^2}\,du = \tan^{-1}(\sin x)$$

**Step 2 — Apply the limit.**
$$\lim_{t \to \infty}\Big[\tan^{-1}(\sin x)\Big]_0^t = \lim_{t \to \infty}\tan^{-1}(\sin t) - 0$$

**Step 3 — Examine the limit.** As $t \to \infty$, $\sin t$ oscillates forever between $-1$ and $1$, so $\tan^{-1}(\sin t)$ oscillates between $-\tfrac{\pi}{4}$ and $\tfrac{\pi}{4}$ and **has no limit**.

## Answer
The limit does not exist, so the integral **diverges**.

## Remark
Divergence does not always mean "grows to infinity" — an integral also diverges when the limit oscillates without settling. This is a favorite conceptual trap.

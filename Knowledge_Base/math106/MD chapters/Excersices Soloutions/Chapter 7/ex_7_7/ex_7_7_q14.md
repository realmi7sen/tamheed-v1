## Problem
Determine whether $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{2} \frac{1}{x^2 + 4} \, dx$ converges or diverges; if it converges, find its value.

## Rule Used
**Definition of the improper integral** + the standard form $\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x^2 + a^2}\,dx = \frac{1}{a}\tan^{-1}\frac{x}{a} + C$ with $a = 2$.

## Solution
**Step 1 — Integrate.**
$$\int \frac{1}{x^2 + 4} \, dx = \frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}\frac{x}{2}$$

**Step 2 — Apply the limit.**
$$\lim_{t \to -\infty}\left[\frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}\frac{x}{2}\right]_t^2 = \frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}(1) - \lim_{t \to -\infty}\frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}\frac{t}{2}$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate.** $\tan^{-1}(1) = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$ and $\tan^{-1}\frac{t}{2} \to -\dfrac{\pi}{2}$ as $t \to -\infty$:
$$= \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{8} + \frac{\pi}{4}$$

## Answer
The integral **converges** and
$$\int_{-\infty}^{2} \frac{1}{x^2 + 4} \, dx = \frac{3\pi}{8}$$

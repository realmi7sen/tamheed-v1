## Problem

Find the derivative without integrating.
$$\frac{d}{dx} \int_0^x \frac{1}{t + 1} \, dt$$

---

## Rule Used

**The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part I)**
If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then the function $g$ defined by
$$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt \quad a \le x \le b$$
is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, and $g'(x) = f(x)$.

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Identify the function $f(t)$**
The integrand inside the definite integral is a function of the dummy variable $t$:
$$f(t) = \frac{1}{t + 1}$$

**Step 2 — Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus**
By Part I of the FTC, taking the derivative with respect to $x$ of the integral from $0$ to $x$ evaluates the integrand at $x$:
$$\frac{d}{dx} \int_0^x f(t) \, dt = f(x)$$
Substitute $x$ for $t$ in the integrand:
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{x + 1}$$

---

## Answer

$$\frac{1}{x + 1}$$

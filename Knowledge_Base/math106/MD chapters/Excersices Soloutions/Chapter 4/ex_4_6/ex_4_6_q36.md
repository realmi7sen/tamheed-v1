## Problem

Evaluate the integral.
$$\int_0^{\pi/3} \frac{\sin x}{\cos^2 x} \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part II)**
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

**Trigonometric Integrals**
$$\int \sec x \tan x \, dx = \sec x + C$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Rewrite the integrand and find the antiderivative $F(x)$**
Use trigonometric identities to express the integrand in a standard form:
$$\frac{\sin x}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{1}{\cos x} \cdot \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} = \sec x \tan x$$
The antiderivative is a standard rule:
$$F(x) = \int \sec x \tan x \, dx = \sec x$$

**Step 2 — Evaluate $F(b) - F(a)$**
Evaluate $F(x)$ at the upper limit $\pi/3$ and the lower limit $0$:
$$\int_0^{\pi/3} \sec x \tan x \, dx = \left[ \sec x \right]_0^{\pi/3}$$

Evaluate at $x = \pi/3$:
$$F\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \sec\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = \frac{1}{\cos(\pi/3)} = \frac{1}{1/2} = 2$$

Evaluate at $x = 0$:
$$F(0) = \sec(0) = \frac{1}{\cos(0)} = \frac{1}{1} = 1$$

**Step 3 — Subtract the results**
$$F\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) - F(0) = 2 - 1 = 1$$

---

## Answer

$$1$$

## Check by Differentiation (Antiderivative)

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \sec x \right] = \sec x \tan x = \frac{1}{\cos x} \cdot \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} = \frac{\sin x}{\cos^2 x} \quad \checkmark$$

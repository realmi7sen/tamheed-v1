## Problem

Evaluate the integral.
$$\int_0^{\pi/2} 3\sin\left(\frac{1}{2}x\right) \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part II)**
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

**Integration of Sine**
$$\int \sin(kx) \, dx = -\frac{1}{k}\cos(kx) + C$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Find the antiderivative $F(x)$**
Apply the integration rule for sine with $k = 1/2$:
$$F(x) = \int 3\sin\left(\frac{1}{2}x\right) \, dx = 3 \left( -\frac{1}{1/2}\cos\left(\frac{1}{2}x\right) \right) = 3 \left( -2\cos\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) \right) = -6\cos\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)$$

**Step 2 — Evaluate $F(b) - F(a)$**
Evaluate $F(x)$ at the upper limit $\pi/2$ and the lower limit $0$:
$$\int_0^{\pi/2} 3\sin\left(\frac{1}{2}x\right) \, dx = \left[ -6\cos\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) \right]_0^{\pi/2}$$

Evaluate at $x = \pi/2$:
$$F\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -6\cos\left(\frac{\pi/2}{2}\right) = -6\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = -6\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) = -3\sqrt{2}$$

Evaluate at $x = 0$:
$$F(0) = -6\cos(0) = -6(1) = -6$$

**Step 3 — Subtract the results**
$$F\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) - F(0) = -3\sqrt{2} - (-6) = 6 - 3\sqrt{2}$$

---

## Answer

$$6 - 3\sqrt{2}$$

---

## Check by Differentiation (Antiderivative)

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ -6\cos\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) \right] = -6\left(-\sin\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)\right) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{x}{2}\right]$$
$$= 6\sin\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 3\sin\left(\frac{1}{2}x\right) \quad \checkmark$$

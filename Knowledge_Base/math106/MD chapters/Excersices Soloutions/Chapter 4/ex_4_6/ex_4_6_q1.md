## Problem

Evaluate the integral.
$$\int_1^4 (x^2 - 4x - 3) \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part II)**
If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$, then:
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

**Power Rule for Integration**
$$\int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Find the antiderivative $F(x)$**
Integrate the polynomial term by term using the power rule:
$$F(x) = \int (x^2 - 4x - 3) \, dx = \frac{x^3}{3} - 4\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right) - 3x = \frac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 - 3x$$

**Step 2 — Evaluate $F(b) - F(a)$**
Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus by evaluating $F(x)$ at the upper limit $4$ and the lower limit $1$:
$$\int_1^4 (x^2 - 4x - 3) \, dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 - 3x \right]_1^4$$

Evaluate at $x = 4$:
$$F(4) = \frac{4^3}{3} - 2(4)^2 - 3(4) = \frac{64}{3} - 32 - 12 = \frac{64}{3} - 44$$
Convert $-44$ to a fraction with a denominator of $3$:
$$F(4) = \frac{64}{3} - \frac{132}{3} = -\frac{68}{3}$$

Evaluate at $x = 1$:
$$F(1) = \frac{1^3}{3} - 2(1)^2 - 3(1) = \frac{1}{3} - 2 - 3 = \frac{1}{3} - 5$$
$$F(1) = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{15}{3} = -\frac{14}{3}$$

**Step 3 — Subtract the results**
$$F(4) - F(1) = -\frac{68}{3} - \left( -\frac{14}{3} \right) = -\frac{68}{3} + \frac{14}{3} = -\frac{54}{3} = -18$$

---

## Answer

$$-18$$

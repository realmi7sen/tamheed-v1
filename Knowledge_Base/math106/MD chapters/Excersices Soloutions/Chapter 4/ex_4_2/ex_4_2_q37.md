## Problem

Evaluate the integral.
$$\int \frac{\sin x}{\cos^4 x} \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**Method of Substitution**
$$\int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du$$

**Power Rule for Integration**
$$\int u^n \, du = \frac{u^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Choose a substitution $u$ and find $du$**
Let $u$ be the base of the power in the denominator:
$$u = \cos x$$
Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:
$$du = -\sin x \, dx \implies \sin x \, dx = -du$$

**Step 2 — Substitute $u$ and $du$ into the integral**
Rewrite the denominator as a negative exponent:
$$\int \frac{1}{\cos^4 x} (\sin x \, dx) = \int \frac{1}{u^4} (-du) = -\int u^{-4} \, du$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate the integral**
Apply the power rule:
$$= -\left( \frac{u^{-3}}{-3} \right) + C = \frac{1}{3}u^{-3} + C = \frac{1}{3u^3} + C$$

**Step 4 — Substitute back the original expression for $x$**
Replace $u$ with $\cos x$:
$$= \frac{1}{3\cos^3 x} + C$$
Which can also be written using the secant function as $\frac{1}{3}\sec^3 x + C$.

---

## Answer

$$\frac{1}{3\cos^3 x} + C$$

---

## Check by Differentiation

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{1}{3}(\cos x)^{-3} + C \right] = \frac{1}{3}(-3)(\cos x)^{-4} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\cos x]$$
$$= -(\cos x)^{-4} \cdot (-\sin x) = \frac{\sin x}{\cos^4 x} \quad \checkmark$$

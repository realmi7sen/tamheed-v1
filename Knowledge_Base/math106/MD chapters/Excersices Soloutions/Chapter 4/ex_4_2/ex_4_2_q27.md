## Problem

Evaluate the integral.
$$\int \cos(4x - 3) \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**Method of Substitution**
$$\int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du$$

**Integration of Cosine**
$$\int \cos u \, du = \sin u + C$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Choose a substitution $u$ and find $du$**
Let $u$ be the inner argument of the cosine function:
$$u = 4x - 3$$
Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:
$$du = 4 \, dx \implies dx = \frac{1}{4} \, du$$

**Step 2 — Substitute $u$ and $du$ into the integral**
$$\int \cos(4x - 3) \, dx = \int \cos(u) \left(\frac{1}{4} \, du\right) = \frac{1}{4} \int \cos u \, du$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate the integral**
The antiderivative of cosine is sine:
$$= \frac{1}{4}\sin u + C$$

**Step 4 — Substitute back the original expression for $x$**
Replace $u$ with $4x - 3$:
$$= \frac{1}{4}\sin(4x - 3) + C$$

---

## Answer

$$\frac{1}{4}\sin(4x - 3) + C$$

---

## Check by Differentiation

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{1}{4}\sin(4x - 3) + C \right] = \frac{1}{4}\cos(4x - 3) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[4x - 3]$$
$$= \frac{1}{4}\cos(4x - 3) \cdot (4) = \cos(4x - 3) \quad \checkmark$$

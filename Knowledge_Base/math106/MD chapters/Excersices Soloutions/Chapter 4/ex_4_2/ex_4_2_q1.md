## Problem

Evaluate the integral using the given substitution, and express the answer in terms of $x$.
$$\int x(2x^2 + 3)^{10} \, dx; \quad u = 2x^2 + 3$$

---

## Rule Used

**Method of Substitution**
$$\int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du$$

**Power Rule for Integration**
$$\int u^n \, du = \frac{u^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Find the differential $du$**
Using the given substitution $u = 2x^2 + 3$, we differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:
$$du = 4x \, dx \implies x \, dx = \frac{1}{4} \, du$$

**Step 2 — Substitute $u$ and $du$ into the integral**
Rewrite the integral in terms of $u$ entirely. Group the $x$ with the $dx$:
$$\int (2x^2 + 3)^{10} (x \, dx) = \int u^{10} \left(\frac{1}{4} \, du\right) = \frac{1}{4} \int u^{10} \, du$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate the integral**
Apply the power rule for integration:
$$= \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{u^{11}}{11} \right) + C = \frac{1}{44}u^{11} + C$$

**Step 4 — Substitute back the original expression for $x$**
Replace $u$ with $2x^2 + 3$ to express the final answer in terms of $x$:
$$= \frac{1}{44}(2x^2 + 3)^{11} + C$$

---

## Answer

$$\frac{1}{44}(2x^2 + 3)^{11} + C$$

---

## Check by Differentiation

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{1}{44}(2x^2 + 3)^{11} + C \right] = \frac{1}{44} \cdot 11(2x^2 + 3)^{10} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[2x^2 + 3] + 0$$
$$= \frac{1}{4}(2x^2 + 3)^{10} \cdot (4x) = x(2x^2 + 3)^{10} \quad \checkmark$$

## Problem

Evaluate the integral using the given substitution, and express the answer in terms of $x$.
$$\int \sqrt{x} \cos \sqrt{x^3} \, dx; \quad u = x^{3/2}$$

---

## Rule Used

**Method of Substitution**
$$\int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du$$

**Integration of Cosine**
$$\int \cos u \, du = \sin u + C$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Find the differential $du$**
Starting with $u = x^{3/2}$, differentiate with respect to $x$:
$$du = \frac{3}{2}x^{1/2} \, dx = \frac{3}{2}\sqrt{x} \, dx$$
Multiply by $\frac{2}{3}$ to isolate $\sqrt{x} \, dx$:
$$\sqrt{x} \, dx = \frac{2}{3} \, du$$

**Step 2 — Substitute $u$ and $du$ into the integral**
Note that $\sqrt{x^3} = (x^3)^{1/2} = x^{3/2} = u$. We group $\sqrt{x}$ with $dx$:
$$\int \cos(\sqrt{x^3}) (\sqrt{x} \, dx) = \int \cos(u) \left(\frac{2}{3} \, du\right) = \frac{2}{3} \int \cos u \, du$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate the integral**
The antiderivative of cosine is sine:
$$= \frac{2}{3}\sin u + C$$

**Step 4 — Substitute back the original expression for $x$**
Replace $u$ with $x^{3/2}$ (or $\sqrt{x^3}$):
$$= \frac{2}{3}\sin(x^{3/2}) + C$$

---

## Answer

$$\frac{2}{3}\sin(x^{3/2}) + C$$

---

## Check by Differentiation

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ \frac{2}{3}\sin(x^{3/2}) + C \right] = \frac{2}{3}\cos(x^{3/2}) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[x^{3/2}]$$
$$= \frac{2}{3}\cos(\sqrt{x^3}) \cdot \left(\frac{3}{2}x^{1/2}\right) = \sqrt{x}\cos\sqrt{x^3} \quad \checkmark$$

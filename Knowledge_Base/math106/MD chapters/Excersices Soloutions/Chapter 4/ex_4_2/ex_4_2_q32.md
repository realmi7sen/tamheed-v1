## Problem

Evaluate the integral.
$$\int \frac{\sin 2x}{\sqrt{1 - \cos 2x}} \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**Method of Substitution**
$$\int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du$$

**Power Rule for Integration**
$$\int u^n \, du = \frac{u^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1)$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Choose a substitution $u$ and find $du$**
Let $u$ be the expression inside the radical in the denominator:
$$u = 1 - \cos 2x$$
Differentiate both sides using the chain rule ($\frac{d}{dx}[\cos(2x)] = -2\sin(2x)$):
$$du = -( -2\sin 2x ) \, dx = 2\sin 2x \, dx$$
Divide by 2 to match the numerator of our integrand:
$$\sin 2x \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \, du$$

**Step 2 — Substitute $u$ and $du$ into the integral**
$$\int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \cos 2x}} (\sin 2x \, dx) = \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{u}} \left(\frac{1}{2} \, du\right)$$
Rewrite the radical as a negative fractional exponent:
$$= \frac{1}{2} \int u^{-1/2} \, du$$

**Step 3 — Evaluate the integral**
Apply the power rule:
$$= \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{u^{1/2}}{1/2} \right) + C = \frac{1}{2}(2\sqrt{u}) + C = \sqrt{u} + C$$

**Step 4 — Substitute back the original expression for $x$**
Replace $u$ with $1 - \cos 2x$:
$$= \sqrt{1 - \cos 2x} + C$$

---

## Answer

$$\sqrt{1 - \cos 2x} + C$$

---

## Check by Differentiation

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[ (1 - \cos 2x)^{1/2} + C \right] = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2x)^{-1/2} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[1 - \cos 2x]$$
$$= \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1 - \cos 2x}} \cdot (2\sin 2x) = \frac{\sin 2x}{\sqrt{1 - \cos 2x}} \quad \checkmark$$

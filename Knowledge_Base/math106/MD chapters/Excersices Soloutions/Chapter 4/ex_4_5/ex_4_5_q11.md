## Problem

Verify the inequality without evaluating the integrals.
$$\int_1^2 (3x^2 + 4) \, dx \ge \int_1^2 (2x^2 + 5) \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**Comparison Theorem for Integrals**
If $f(x) \ge g(x)$ for all $x$ in $[a, b]$, then $\int_a^b f(x) \, dx \ge \int_a^b g(x) \, dx$.

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Define the functions and set up an inequality**
Let $f(x) = 3x^2 + 4$ and $g(x) = 2x^2 + 5$.
We want to show that $f(x) \ge g(x)$ on the interval $[1, 2]$. Let's examine the difference $f(x) - g(x)$:
$$f(x) - g(x) = (3x^2 + 4) - (2x^2 + 5) = x^2 - 1$$

**Step 2 — Analyze the difference on the given interval**
The interval of integration is $[1, 2]$.
For any $x$ in $[1, 2]$, we know that $x \ge 1$.
Squaring both sides gives $x^2 \ge 1$, which means $x^2 - 1 \ge 0$.

**Step 3 — Apply the Comparison Theorem**
Since $x^2 - 1 \ge 0$, it follows that $f(x) - g(x) \ge 0$, or equivalently:
$$3x^2 + 4 \ge 2x^2 + 5$$
Because this inequality holds for all $x$ in $[1, 2]$, by the Comparison Theorem for integrals, it must also be true that:
$$\int_1^2 (3x^2 + 4) \, dx \ge \int_1^2 (2x^2 + 5) \, dx$$

---

## Answer

The inequality is verified because $3x^2 + 4 \ge 2x^2 + 5$ for all $x$ in $[1, 2]$.

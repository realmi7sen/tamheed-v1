## Problem

Express as one integral.
$$\int_c^m f(x) \, dx - \int_d^m f(x) \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**Reversing Limits of Integration**
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = -\int_b^a f(x) \, dx$$

**Additive Interval Property**
$$\int_a^b f(x) \, dx + \int_b^c f(x) \, dx = \int_a^c f(x) \, dx$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Reverse the limits of the second integral**
The negative sign in front of the second integral can be absorbed by swapping its upper and lower limits:
$$- \int_d^m f(x) \, dx = + \int_m^d f(x) \, dx$$

**Step 2 — Rewrite the expression**
Substitute the reversed integral back into the original expression:
$$\int_c^m f(x) \, dx + \int_m^d f(x) \, dx$$

**Step 3 — Apply the Additive Interval Property**
Notice that the upper limit of the first integral ($m$) matches the lower limit of the second integral ($m$). This allows us to combine them into a single integral from $c$ to $d$:
$$= \int_c^d f(x) \, dx$$

---

## Answer

$$\int_c^d f(x) \, dx$$

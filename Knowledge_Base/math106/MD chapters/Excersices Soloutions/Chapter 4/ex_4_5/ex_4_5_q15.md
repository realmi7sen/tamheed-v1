## Problem

Verify the inequality without evaluating the integrals.
$$\int_0^{2\pi} (1 + \sin x) \, dx \ge 0$$

---

## Rule Used

**Positivity Property of Integrals**
If $f(x) \ge 0$ for all $x$ in $[a, b]$, then $\int_a^b f(x) \, dx \ge 0$.

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Analyze the integrand**
Let $f(x) = 1 + \sin x$.
We need to determine the range of values for this function on the interval $[0, 2\pi]$.

**Step 2 — Use the properties of the sine function**
We know that for any real number $x$, the sine function is bounded between $-1$ and $1$:
$$-1 \le \sin x \le 1$$

**Step 3 — Establish the inequality for the integrand**
Add $1$ to all parts of the inequality:
$$-1 + 1 \le 1 + \sin x \le 1 + 1$$
$$0 \le 1 + \sin x \le 2$$
This shows that $f(x) = 1 + \sin x \ge 0$ for all real numbers $x$, including the interval $[0, 2\pi]$.

**Step 4 — Apply the property of integrals**
Since the integrand is non-negative on $[0, 2\pi]$, the integral of the function over this interval must also be non-negative:
$$\int_0^{2\pi} (1 + \sin x) \, dx \ge 0$$

---

## Answer

The inequality is verified because $1 + \sin x \ge 0$ for all $x$ in $[0, 2\pi]$.

## Problem

Given $\int_1^4 \sqrt{x} \, dx = \frac{14}{3}$, evaluate the integral.
$$\int_4^1 \sqrt{x} \, dx$$

---

## Rule Used

**Reversing Limits of Integration**
If the upper and lower limits of a definite integral are interchanged, the sign of the integral is reversed:
$$\int_b^a f(x) \, dx = -\int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Apply the rule for reversing limits**
The given integral goes from $1$ to $4$, while the requested integral goes from $4$ to $1$. 
$$\int_4^1 \sqrt{x} \, dx = -\int_1^4 \sqrt{x} \, dx$$

**Step 2 — Substitute the known value**
Since we are given that $\int_1^4 \sqrt{x} \, dx = \frac{14}{3}$, we substitute this directly into our equation:
$$-\left( \frac{14}{3} \right)$$

---

## Answer

$$-\frac{14}{3}$$

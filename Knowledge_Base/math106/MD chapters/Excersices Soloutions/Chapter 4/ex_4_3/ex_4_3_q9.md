## Problem

Express the sum in terms of $n$.
$$\sum_{k=1}^n (k^2 + 3k + 5)$$

---

## Rule Used

**Properties of Sums**
$$\sum_{k=1}^n (a_k + b_k) = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k + \sum_{k=1}^n b_k$$
$$\sum_{k=1}^n c a_k = c \sum_{k=1}^n a_k$$

**Summation Formulas**
$$\sum_{k=1}^n c = cn$$
$$\sum_{k=1}^n k = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$$
$$\sum_{k=1}^n k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$$

---

## Solution

**Step 1 — Separate the sum using linearity properties**
Break the sum into three distinct summations and pull out constants:
$$\sum_{k=1}^n (k^2 + 3k + 5) = \sum_{k=1}^n k^2 + 3\sum_{k=1}^n k + \sum_{k=1}^n 5$$

**Step 2 — Apply the summation formulas**
Substitute the known formulas for the sums of $k^2$, $k$, and constants:
$$= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + 3\left[ \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \right] + 5n$$

**Step 3 — Find a common denominator to combine the terms**
The common denominator for $6$, $2$, and $1$ is $6$:
$$= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + \frac{9n(n+1)}{6} + \frac{30n}{6}$$
$$= \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1) + 9n(n+1) + 30n}{6}$$

**Step 4 — Factor and expand to simplify**
Factor out the common $n$ from the numerator:
$$= \frac{n}{6} \left[ (n+1)(2n+1) + 9(n+1) + 30 \right]$$
Expand the terms inside the brackets:
$$= \frac{n}{6} \left[ (2n^2 + 3n + 1) + (9n + 9) + 30 \right]$$
Combine like terms:
$$= \frac{n}{6} \left[ 2n^2 + 12n + 40 \right]$$

**Step 5 — Final simplification**
Factor out a $2$ from the quadratic expression:
$$= \frac{n}{6} \cdot 2(n^2 + 6n + 20) = \frac{n(n^2 + 6n + 20)}{3}$$

---

## Answer

$$\frac{n(n^2 + 6n + 20)}{3}$$

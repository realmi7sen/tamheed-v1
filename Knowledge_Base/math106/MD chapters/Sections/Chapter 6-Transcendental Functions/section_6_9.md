## Theorems[cite: 9]
**Cauchy's Formula 6.50**[cite: 9]
If f and g are continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$ and if $g^{\prime}(x)\ne0$ for every x in $(a,b)$, then there is a number w in $(a,b)$ such that[cite: 9]
$$\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}=\frac{f^{\prime}(w)}{g^{\prime}(w)}.$$[cite: 9]

**l'Hôpital's Rule 6.51**[cite: 9]
Suppose that f and g are differentiable on an open interval $(a,b)$ containing c, except possibly at c itself. If $f(x)/g(x)$ has the indeterminate form $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$ at $x=c$ and if $g^{\prime}(x)\ne0$ for $x\ne c$ then[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow c}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=lim_{x\rightarrow c}\frac{f^{\prime}(x)}{g^{\prime}(x)},$$[cite: 9]
provided either[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow c}\frac{f^{\prime}(x)}{g^{\prime}(x)}$$[cite: 9]
exists or $lim_{x\rightarrow c}\frac{f^{\prime}(x)}{g^{\prime}(x)}=\infty$[cite: 9]

## Guidelines / Methods[cite: 9]
**Guidelines for Investigating $lim_{x\rightarrow c}[f(x)g(x)]$ for the Form $0\cdot\infty$ 6.52**[cite: 9]
1. Write $f(x)g(x)$ as[cite: 9]
$$\frac{f(x)}{1/g(x)} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{g(x)}{1/f(x)}$$[cite: 9]
2. Apply l'Hôpital's rule (6.51) to the resulting indeterminate form $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty.$[cite: 9]

**Guidelines for Investigating $lim_{x\rightarrow c}f(x)^{g(x)}$ for the Forms $0^{0}$ and $\infty^{0}$ 6.53**[cite: 9]
1. Let $y=f(x)^{g(x)}$[cite: 9]
2. Take natural logarithms in guideline (1):[cite: 9]
$$ln~y=ln~f(x)^{g(x)}=g(x)ln~f(x)$$[cite: 9]
3. Investigate $lim_{x\rightarrow c}ln~y=lim_{x\rightarrow c}[g(x)ln~f(x)]$ and conclude the following:[cite: 9]
    (a) If $lim_{x\rightarrow c}ln~y=L$ , then $lim_{x\rightarrow c}y=e^{L}$.[cite: 9]
    (b) If $lim_{x\rightarrow c}ln~y=\infty$ then $lim_{x\rightarrow c}y=\infty$ .[cite: 9]
    (c) If $lim_{x\rightarrow c}ln~y=-\infty$, then $lim_{x\rightarrow c}y=0$ .[cite: 9]

## Worked Examples[cite: 9]
### Example 1[cite: 9]
Find[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{cos~x+2x-1}{3x}.$$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
Both the numerator and the denominator have the limit 0 as $x\rightarrow0$ Hence the quotient has the indeterminate form $0/0$ at $x=0$. By l'Hôpital's rule (6.51),[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{cos~x+2x-1}{3x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{-sin~x+2}{3},$$[cite: 9]
provided the limit on the right exists or equals $\infty$ Since[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{-sin~x+2}{3}=\frac{2}{3},$$[cite: 9]
it follows that[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{cos~x+2x-1}{3x}=\frac{2}{3}$$[cite: 9]

### Example 2[cite: 9]
Find $lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}-2}{1-cos~2x}$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The given quotient has the indeterminate form 0/0. By l'Hôpital's rule,[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}-2}{1-cos~2x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}-e^{-x}}{2~sin~2x}$$[cite: 9]
provided the second limit exists. Because the last quotient has the indeterminate form $0/0,$ we apply l'Hôpital's rule a second time, obtaining[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}-e^{-x}}{2~sin~2x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{4~cos~2x}=\frac{2}{4}=\frac{1}{2}.$$[cite: 9]
It follows that the given limit exists and equals $\frac{1}{2}.$[cite: 9]

### Example 3[cite: 9]
Find $lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{4~tan~x}{1+sec~x}$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The indeterminate form is $\infty/\infty$. By l'Hôpital's rule,[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{4~tan~x}{1+sec~x}=lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{4~sec^{2}x}{sec~x~tan~x}=lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{4~sec~x}{tan~x}.$$[cite: 9]
The last quotient again has the indeterminate form $\infty/\infty$ at $x=\pi/2$; however, additional applications of l'Hôpital's rule always produce the form $\infty/\infty$. In this case, the limit may be found by using trigonometric identities to change the quotient as follows:[cite: 9]
$$\frac{4~sec~x}{tan~x}=\frac{4/cos~x}{sin~x/cos~x}=\frac{4}{sin~x}$$[cite: 9]
Consequently,[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{4~tan~x}{1+sec~x}=lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{4}{sin~x}=\frac{4}{1}=4.$$[cite: 9]

### Example 4[cite: 9]
Find $lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{ln~x}{\sqrt{x}}$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The indeterminate form is $\infty/\infty$. By l'Hôpital's rule,[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{ln~x}{\sqrt{x}}=lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1/x}{1/(2\sqrt{x})}$$[cite: 9]
The last expression has the indeterminate form $0/0$. However, further applications of l'Hôpital's rule would again lead to $0/0$. If, instead, we simplify the expression algebraically, we can find the limit as follows:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1/x}{1/(2\sqrt{x})}=lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{2\sqrt{x}}{x}=lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{2}{\sqrt{x}}=0$$[cite: 9]

### Example 5[cite: 9]
Find $lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{e^{3x}}{x^{2}},$ if it exists.[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The indeterminate form is $\infty/\infty.$ We apply l'Hôpital's rule:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{e^{3x}}{x^{2}}=lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{3e^{3x}}{2x}$$[cite: 9]
The last quotient has the indeterminate form $\infty/\infty,$ so we apply l'Hôpital's rule a second time, obtaining[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{3e^{3x}}{2x}=lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}\frac{9e^{3x}}{2}=\infty$$[cite: 9]
Thus, $e^{3x}/x^{2}$ has no limit, increasing without bound as $x\rightarrow\infty$[cite: 9]

### Example 6[cite: 9]
Find[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{x^{2}}$$[cite: 9]
if it exists.[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The quotient does not have either of the indeterminate forms, $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty,$ at $x=0$ To investigate the limit, we write[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{x^{2}}=lim_{x\rightarrow0}(e^{x}+e^{-x})(\frac{1}{x^{2}})$$[cite: 9]
Since[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}(e^{x}+e^{-x})=2 \quad \text{and} \quad lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{1}{x^{2}}=\infty,$$[cite: 9]
it follows that[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{x^{2}}=\infty.$$[cite: 9]

### Example 8[cite: 9]
Find $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}x^{2}ln~x.$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The indeterminate form is $0\cdot\infty$. Applying guideline (1) of (6.52), we write[cite: 9]
$$x^{2}ln~x=\frac{ln~x}{1/x^{2}}.$$[cite: 9]
Because the quotient on the right has the indeterminate form $\infty/\infty$ at $x=0,$ we may apply l'Hôpital's rule:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}x^{2}ln~x=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{ln~x}{1/x^{2}}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{1/x}{-2/x^{3}}$$[cite: 9]
The last quotient has the indeterminate form $\infty/\infty$; however, further applications of l'Hôpital's rule would again lead to $\infty/\infty.$ In this case, we simplify the quotient algebraically and find the limit as follows:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{1/x}{-2/x^{3}}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{x^{3}}{-2x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{x^{2}}{-2}=0$$[cite: 9]

### Example 9[cite: 9]
Find $lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}(2x-\pi)sec~x$ .[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The indeterminate form is $0\cdot\infty$ Using guideline (1) of (6.52), we begin by writing[cite: 9]
$$(2x-\pi)sec~x=\frac{2x-\pi}{1/sec~x}=\frac{2x-\pi}{cos~x}.$$[cite: 9]
Because the last expression has the indeterminate form $0/0$ at $x=\pi/2,$ l'Hôpital's rule may be applied as follows:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{2x-\pi}{cos~x}=lim_{x\rightarrow(\pi/2)^{-}}\frac{2}{-sin~x}=\frac{2}{-1}=-2$$[cite: 9]

### Example 10[cite: 9]
Find[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}x^{x}$$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The indeterminate form is $0^{0}.$ Using Guidelines (6.53), we proceed as follows:[cite: 9]
Guideline 1: $y=x^{x}$[cite: 9]
Guideline 2: $ln~y=x~ln~x$[cite: 9]
Guideline 3: This expression has the indeterminate form $0\cdot\infty$ We apply guideline (1) of (6.52) to write[cite: 9]
$$x~ln~x=\frac{ln~x}{1/x}$$[cite: 9]
Since the quotient on the right has the indeterminate form $\infty/\infty$ at $x=0$, we may apply l'Hôpital's rule:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}ln~y=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}x~ln~x=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{ln~x}{1/x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{1/x}{-1/x^{2}}$$[cite: 9]
We can evaluate this last limit by an algebraic simplification:[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{1/x}{-1/x^{2}}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}(-\frac{x^{2}}{x})=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}(-x)=0$$[cite: 9]
Since $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}~ln~y=0$, by guideline (3a) of (6.53), we have[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}x^{x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}y=e^{0}=1$$ .[cite: 9]

### Example 11[cite: 9]
Find[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}(\frac{1}{e^{x}-1}-\frac{1}{x})$$[cite: 9]
**Solution**[cite: 9]
The form is $\infty-\infty$ however, if the difference is written as a single fraction, then[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}(\frac{1}{e^{x}-1}-\frac{1}{x})=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{x-e^{x}+1}{xe^{x}-x}.$$[cite: 9]
This gives us the indeterminate form $0/0$. It is necessary to apply l'Hôpital's rule twice, since the first application leads to the indeterminate form $0/0$. Thus,[cite: 9]
$$lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{x-e^{x}+1}{xe^{x}-x}=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{1-e^{x}}{xe^{x}+e^{x}-1}$$[cite: 9]
$$=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}\frac{-e^{x}}{xe^{x}+2e^{x}}=-\frac{1}{2}$$[cite: 9]
